# Software Architecture Patterns

## Introduction

Software architecture defines the high-level structure of a software system and encompasses the set of significant design decisions about the organization of a system, including the selection of structural elements, their interfaces, the behavior of those elements in collaboration, and the composition of those elements into progressively larger subsystems [Bass et al., 2012]. Modern systems face competing requirements: they must be highly available, horizontally scalable, independently deployable, and maintainable by large engineering teams. No single architectural style satisfies all these demands simultaneously. Instead, architects select and combine patterns based on team size, deployment infrastructure, consistency requirements, and expected traffic patterns. This document surveys six major architectural styles and provides structured comparison criteria and implementation guidance for teams choosing or migrating between them.

## Architectural Patterns Comparison

Selecting the right architectural pattern requires evaluating multiple quality attributes simultaneously. Column definitions used in the comparison table: *Horizontal Scalability* refers to the ability to add instances to handle increased load without redesigning the system; *Fault Isolation* describes the degree to which a failure in one component is prevented from propagating to others; *Deployment Complexity* covers the operational skill and tooling required to release changes reliably to production; *Development Speed* measures the time-to-feature for a typical engineering team over the first twelve months of a project; *Technology Overhead* captures the infrastructure cost imposed by the pattern itself rather than by application logic; *Team Autonomy* reflects how independently teams can release, own, and operate their components without coordinating with other teams; *Data Consistency* describes the strongest consistency guarantee achievable across the system's persistent storage tier.

|------------------|--------------------------------------|----------------------------------------------|---------------------------------|---------------------------------|--------------------------------------------|---------------|--------------------------|
| Monolithic       | Limited to vertical scaling only     | Poor — single process crash affects all      | Low — one artifact to deploy    | Fast for small teams (<10 devs) | None — standard runtime only               | Low           | Strong ACID via one DB   |
| Microservices    | Excellent — scale per service        | Excellent — failure contained per service    | High — per-service CI/CD needed | Slow setup; fast iteration later| Moderate — service discovery, LB, registry | High          | Eventual by default      |
| Event-Driven     | Excellent — brokers absorb backpressure| Good — producers and consumers decouple    | High — requires message broker infrastructure | Medium — event schemas add ceremony | High — broker ops, schema registry | Medium   | Eventual; saga required  |
| Serverless       | Automatic — managed by platform      | Good — functions isolated by default         | Low — deploy code only          | Very fast — zero infra setup    | Significant — vendor lock-in risk          | High          | Depends on backing store |
| Service Mesh     | Same as underlying services          | Excellent — policy enforced at proxy layer   | Very High — control plane ops   | Unchanged from base services    | Significant — requires sidecar proxies     | Medium        | Same as underlying       |

The table above presents a point-in-time comparison across eight dimensions; real-world trade-offs emerge over months of operation as team structure, traffic patterns, and data volume evolve in ways that initial architecture decisions must anticipate rather than merely react to. Organizations frequently underestimate how much their current team size, domain knowledge, and operational maturity constrain the set of realistically achievable architectures. A pattern that looks ideal on paper becomes an operational liability when the team lacks the skills to run it effectively; equally, a pattern that appears limiting can be highly productive when the team can execute it with confidence and speed. Architecture is not a one-time decision but an ongoing series of adjustments made in response to observed system behaviour and changing organizational needs.

The monolithic architecture, despite its reputation as an anti-pattern in modern discourse, remains the appropriate starting point for the majority of new products. Conway's Law states that organizations design systems that mirror their communication structures; a small founding team will naturally produce a cohesive, low-ceremony monolith. The overhead of splitting a system into independently deployable services before domain boundaries are well-understood leads to premature abstractions that calcify incorrect service boundaries, making later migration more expensive than the original monolith ever was. Fowler and Lewis recommended that organizations begin with a monolith and extract services only after identifying stable, high-load subsystems with clear ownership boundaries [Fowler, 2015]. The monolith's greatest strength — a single shared process and a single database — becomes its greatest weakness only at significant scale: when a single team can no longer own the entire codebase, when deployments must be serialized across too many teams, or when one component's resource consumption starves others. Until those conditions are met, the monolith is almost always the correct choice because it offers the fastest path from idea to production, the simplest debugging experience, and the lowest operational surface area.

The circuit breaker pattern, introduced by Michael Nygard in Release It! (2007), prevents cascading failures by tracking failure rates over a rolling time window of sixty seconds. When the failure rate exceeds a configured threshold — typically 50% of calls within the window — the circuit transitions from Closed to Open, immediately returning an error to callers without attempting the downstream call. After a configured reset timeout, the circuit enters a Half-Open state: one probe request is sent, and if it succeeds the circuit returns to Closed; if it fails the circuit returns to Open for another full timeout. This pattern is essential in microservices deployments where a slow or failing downstream service can exhaust the thread pool and connection pool of every upstream caller within minutes, triggering a cascading failure that takes down the entire application. Production experience with large microservices deployments consistently shows that missing circuit breakers is the leading cause of total availability loss during partial infrastructure failures, even when individual services have health checks and restart policies configured correctly.

When adopting microservices, the strangler fig pattern allows incremental migration by routing new functionality to the new service while legacy routes remain in the monolith until fully replaced. The pattern takes its name from the strangler fig tree, which grows around a host tree over decades, eventually replacing it entirely while the host continues to function. In practice, an API gateway or reverse proxy sits in front of both the monolith and the growing set of microservices, routing requests by path prefix or header value. The critical discipline is to migrate one bounded context at a time, verify the new service in production with real traffic before deleting the corresponding monolith code, and maintain backward-compatible APIs throughout the transition period. Teams that skip this discipline and attempt to migrate two bounded contexts simultaneously consistently report integration failures that delay the overall migration timeline by months. The strangler fig pattern also provides a natural rollback mechanism: if the new service misbehaves in production, the API gateway can redirect its routes back to the monolith while the team investigates, without requiring a full redeploy of the monolith itself.

Event-driven architecture requires explicit handling of message ordering, idempotency, and schema evolution at the design phase rather than as afterthoughts discovered during debugging. A consumer that processes the same message twice due to a network retry must produce the same observable outcome both times; this idempotency requirement affects database schema design, external API call patterns, and cache invalidation logic in non-obvious ways. Kafka's log-compacted topics provide a useful primitive for maintaining idempotent state: by keying messages by entity identifier and enabling log compaction, the broker retains only the most recent message per key, allowing consumers that restart to replay only the current state rather than the full event history. Schema registry integration — using Apache Avro, Protocol Buffers, or JSON Schema — enforces schema compatibility rules at publish time, preventing producers from introducing fields or removing required fields in ways that would break existing consumers without a coordinated cross-team rollout. The combination of log compaction and schema enforcement makes Kafka a compelling choice for event-driven systems that require both durability and evolvability, at the cost of significantly higher operational complexity than a simple message queue.

Serverless functions are best suited to workloads with irregular, spiky traffic patterns where paying for idle compute is economically wasteful. The primary operational constraint is cold start latency: when a function has not been invoked recently, the platform must provision a new execution environment, download the function code and its dependencies, and initialize the runtime before processing the first request. This cold start latency of 50 to 500 milliseconds per cold invocation is acceptable for background jobs, event-driven processing, and webhook handlers, but it disqualifies serverless from latency-sensitive synchronous APIs where p99 latency targets are below 100 milliseconds. Keeping functions warm through scheduled ping invocations addresses cold starts at the cost of eliminating the primary economic benefit of serverless: paying only for actual executions. The practical recommendation is to use serverless for asynchronous tasks — image resizing, report generation, notification dispatch, data pipeline stages — and conventional containerized services for latency-sensitive synchronous API paths. Memory allocation also affects cold start duration; functions allocated 512 MB or more consistently exhibit shorter cold start times than those allocated 128 MB, because the platform provisions proportionally more CPU for initialization.

Service mesh adoption should be driven by observable operational problems rather than architectural aspiration or industry trend. The operational overhead of a mesh control plane — managing certificate rotation, policy distribution, and telemetry aggregation across hundreds of sidecar proxies — is substantial and requires dedicated platform engineering expertise. Teams that adopt a service mesh primarily for mutual TLS encryption between services often discover that the same goal can be achieved with significantly less operational complexity through Kubernetes Network Policy and RBAC at the namespace boundary. The strongest cases for a service mesh arise when an organization needs uniform observability — distributed tracing, per-service error rate dashboards, and latency histograms — across services owned by many different teams who cannot be relied upon to instrument their own code consistently, and when advanced traffic management such as weighted routing, fault injection, and circuit breaking at the proxy layer is required without modifying service source code. In those circumstances, the operational overhead of the mesh is justified by the elimination of per-service instrumentation and traffic management code that would otherwise drift in consistency across dozens of teams.

The layered (N-tier) architecture separates an application into horizontal tiers — typically presentation, business logic, and data access — where each tier communicates only with the tier directly below it. This separation promotes testability: the business logic tier can be unit-tested without a database by substituting a mock data access layer, and the presentation tier can be tested without invoking real business logic through a mock service interface. The primary scalability limitation of the layered architecture is the shared database tier, which acts as a single point of contention as the application grows: all tiers share the same database connection pool, and schema changes require coordination across all tiers simultaneously. Teams working with layered architectures can extend their lifespan by partitioning the database horizontally (read replicas for read-heavy tiers, separate schemas for logically distinct domains) and by applying the CQRS pattern to separate read and write models. These extensions add complexity but can postpone the need for a full architectural migration by several years.

Choosing the wrong architectural pattern is expensive but recoverable. The most common migration paths — monolith to microservices, layered to event-driven, synchronous to asynchronous — are well-documented and achievable incrementally by teams that commit to strangler fig discipline, invest in observability from the first day of the migration, and maintain a clear mapping between domain concepts and service boundaries throughout the transition. Organizations that rush migrations to hit an architectural deadline without those disciplines consistently produce microservices architectures that are harder to operate than the monoliths they replaced: distributed monoliths where all services must be deployed together, where a failure in any one service takes down the whole system, and where the service boundaries were drawn by technology categories rather than by domain ownership. Taking the time to understand domain boundaries — through event storming sessions with domain experts, through careful analysis of change frequency across the monolith's modules, and through explicit team topology discussions — before drawing service boundaries is the single highest-value investment in a migration. Architecture governance programs that review and document every significant architectural decision — recording the forces that led to the decision, the options that were considered, the option chosen, and the rationale — create organizational memory that prevents teams from relitigating settled decisions and helps new engineers understand why the system is structured the way it is.

## Microservices Communication Patterns

Microservices communicate through two primary mechanisms: synchronous request-response (typically HTTP/REST or gRPC) and asynchronous messaging (typically via Kafka, RabbitMQ, or cloud-native queues such as Amazon SQS or Google Pub/Sub). The choice between synchronous and asynchronous communication has far-reaching implications for availability, consistency, and developer experience that are difficult to reverse once established.

Synchronous communication is simpler to reason about and debug: a caller sends a request and waits for a response within a bounded timeout. The coupling is temporal: if the downstream service is unavailable or slow, the call fails or degrades. This coupling can be mitigated with circuit breakers and retry budgets with exponential backoff and jitter, but it cannot be eliminated without introducing asynchrony. Synchronous communication works well for operations where the caller needs an immediate confirmation (payment processing, user authentication, inventory reservation) and where the acceptable latency budget accommodates the round-trip time to the downstream service.

Asynchronous messaging decouples producers from consumers in time: a producer publishes a message to a broker and continues immediately, regardless of whether any consumer is available. This temporal decoupling improves availability and enables backpressure absorption at the broker rather than cascading back to the producer, but it complicates end-to-end distributed tracing, error handling, and business transaction coordination. The saga pattern addresses distributed transaction management in fully asynchronous microservices: a saga is a sequence of local transactions, each publishing an event that triggers the next local transaction in another service, with compensating transactions defined for each step to handle rollback scenarios.
