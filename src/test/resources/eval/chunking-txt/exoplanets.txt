The search for planets beyond our solar system stands as one of the defining scientific pursuits of the past three decades. Before 1992 the existence of planets around other stars was purely theoretical, inferred from planetary science but never directly confirmed. The discovery of a pair of planets orbiting a pulsar by Aleksander Wolszczan and Dale Frail that year, followed by the first confirmed planet around a sun-like star detected by Michel Mayor and Didier Queloz in 1995, opened an entirely new era of astronomical observation. Today astronomers have confirmed more than five thousand exoplanets spanning an enormous range of masses, radii, orbital periods, and host star types, and the field has shifted from mere detection to detailed characterization of planetary environments.

The radial velocity method, which detects the Doppler wobble a planet induces in its host star, was the dominant detection technique through the first two decades of exoplanet science. As a planet orbits its star, gravitational attraction causes the star to trace a small ellipse around the common centre of mass, producing a periodic blueshift followed by a redshift in the stellar spectrum. Measuring this velocity variation with high-resolution spectrographs capable of detecting shifts of one metre per second or less allows astronomers to determine the orbital period, the orbital eccentricity, and a lower bound on the planetary mass. This method favours massive planets in short-period orbits but has been applied successfully to systems with multiple low-mass planets through years of patient monitoring, yielding precise dynamical masses that are essential for bulk density calculations.

Transit photometry has since become the most prolific exoplanet detection method, responsible for the majority of confirmed planets in the current catalogue. When a planet passes in front of its host star from the perspective of the observer, it blocks a fraction of the starlight proportional to the square of the planetary radius relative to the stellar radius. By measuring the precise depth and duration of these periodic dimmings, astronomers can determine the planetary radius, the orbital inclination, and the orbital period. The Kepler space telescope, operating from 2009 to 2018, discovered more than twenty-six hundred planets using this technique, revealing that small rocky planets are extraordinarily common in the galaxy, with occurrence rates suggesting that most sun-like stars harbour at least one planet within the habitable zone.

The characterization of exoplanet atmospheres represents the frontier of the field, as it holds the potential to detect signs of habitability or even biological activity on worlds beyond our solar system. When a planet transits its host star, a small fraction of the starlight filters through the planetary atmosphere and carries the imprints of molecular absorption features at specific wavelengths. By comparing the effective radius of the planet at different wavelengths using transmission spectroscopy, astronomers can identify the chemical species responsible for those absorptions. Carbon dioxide, water vapour, methane, ozone, and sodium have all been detected in exoplanet atmospheres, and each molecular signature constrains the temperature structure, cloud properties, and chemical composition of the atmosphere.

Direct imaging of exoplanets remains technically challenging because the contrast between a planet and its host star is enormous, often exceeding a billion to one in visible light. Coronagraphs and starshade designs suppress the stellar glare to allow faint reflected or thermally emitted light from nearby planets to be detected, but current technology limits direct imaging largely to young, massive, self-luminous planets in wide orbits where the angular separation from the star is large enough to resolve. Nonetheless, direct imaging provides spectra with much higher signal-to-noise than transmission or emission spectroscopy because the planet is observed in isolation rather than as a tiny perturbation to the vastly brighter star. Future large space telescopes with extreme coronagraphs are expected to extend direct imaging to true earth twins orbiting sun-like stars. Ground-based high-contrast imaging using adaptive optics systems on telescopes such as the Very Large Telescope and the Gemini Planet Imager has confirmed the orbital motion of directly imaged planets by measuring changes in their sky position over successive years, and their near-infrared spectra reveal methane, water, and complex cloud structures in young giant planet atmospheres at temperatures between five hundred and two thousand kelvin.

The TRAPPIST-1 system discovered by the TRAPPIST telescope in 2017 became the most intensively studied system in all of exoplanet science because it contains seven approximately Earth-size planets orbiting within less than twelve days of a nearby ultracool dwarf star, with three of them residing in the conservative habitable zone. The proximity of the star, at only twelve light-years from Earth, and the small size of the host star relative to its planets make the TRAPPIST-1 worlds the best available targets for atmospheric characterization with current and near-future facilities. Extended photometric monitoring across multiple wavelengths has revealed the precise radii, masses, and bulk densities of all seven worlds, demonstrating that the inner planets are likely rocky and the outer planets may retain significant water content. Transit timing variation measurements have refined the orbital architecture to extraordinary precision, enabling dynamical stability analyses that constrain internal structure models for each planet in the system. TRAPPIST-1e, the fifth planet in the system and one of the three in the habitable zone, has attracted particular attention because its equilibrium temperature is most similar to that of Earth under assumptions of Earth-like albedo and atmospheric greenhouse forcing, and TRAPPIST-1e receives approximately sixty-six percent of the stellar flux that Earth receives from the Sun, placing it in a position analogous to that of early Mars in our own solar system when Mars may have retained liquid surface water.

The James Webb Space Telescope, launched in December 2021 and achieving full science operations by mid-2022, has dramatically expanded the reach of exoplanet atmospheric characterization. Its four science instruments collectively cover wavelengths from approximately one to twenty-eight micrometres, encompassing the primary absorption features of water, carbon dioxide, methane, ozone, ammonia, and sulphur dioxide. Early results from transmission spectroscopy observations of the TRAPPIST-1 planets have constrained atmospheric scenarios and shown that thick hydrogen-dominated atmospheres are ruled out for several of the inner planets, though the detection of secondary atmospheres produced by volcanic outgassing or delivered by cometary bombardment remains an open question. For Earth-size planets around quiet stars like TRAPPIST-1, detecting water vapour in an Earth-size exoplanet atmosphere requires a minimum of fifty transits with the James Webb Space Telescope to achieve a five-sigma detection confidence, a demanding but achievable observational programme that will unfold over the coming decade of JWST operations.

The statistical study of exoplanet populations has revealed several striking patterns that constrain theories of planet formation and migration. The radius gap, a pronounced deficit of planets with radii between approximately one point five and two Earth radii, divides the observed population into two groups, super-Earths below the gap and sub-Neptunes above it. Photoevaporation models and core-powered mass-loss models both predict this bimodality, arising from the stripping of hydrogen-helium envelopes by stellar irradiation or by heat from the cooling planetary interior over gigayear timescales. The hot Jupiter desert, a scarcity of Saturn-mass planets at orbital periods between three and ten days around sun-like stars, hints at a dynamic migration history in which planets that arrive on short-period orbits are either massive enough to resist evaporation or are stripped entirely. Understanding these population patterns requires precisely determined planetary and stellar properties, making the synergy between space photometry and ground-based spectroscopy essential to progress in the field.

The long-term goal of exoplanet science is the detection and characterization of true Earth analogues orbiting in the habitable zones of sun-like stars, and then the search for spectroscopic biosignatures that would provide evidence of life. Proposed biosignature gases include oxygen, ozone, methane in disequilibrium with oxygen, nitrous oxide, and dimethyl sulphide. Each of these gases can in principle be produced abiotically, so establishing a robust biosignature requires measuring multiple gases simultaneously to construct a context that is inconsistent with purely geochemical or photochemical explanations. The most promising near-term detection targets are the TRAPPIST-1 habitable zone planets and other systems around nearby ultracool dwarfs, while the longer-term programme involves ambitious large-aperture telescopes that can achieve the photon-collecting area and coronagraphic contrast needed to characterize Earth twins in reflected starlight.
