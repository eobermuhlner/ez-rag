Stars are not eternal. They are born from vast clouds of gas and dust, live out their lives in a delicate balance between gravity and radiation pressure, and eventually exhaust their nuclear fuel, leaving behind compact remnants or disappearing entirely. Understanding stellar evolution is one of the central achievements of modern astrophysics, linking nuclear physics, thermodynamics, and fluid dynamics into a coherent account of how galaxies generate and recycle their chemical content. Nearly all atoms heavier than hydrogen and helium in your body were created inside a star and scattered across interstellar space when that star died, making stellar evolution inseparable from the history of life itself.

The process of stellar formation begins inside giant molecular clouds, which are enormous cold reservoirs of hydrogen and helium gas, typically ten to one hundred light-years across and containing tens of thousands of solar masses of material. When a region of such a cloud becomes gravitationally unstable, often triggered by the shock wave from a nearby supernova explosion or by tidal forces during a galactic arm crossing, it begins to fragment and collapse. Each fragment spins faster as it contracts, conserving angular momentum, and the core temperature rises as gravitational potential energy converts to heat. When the central temperature reaches roughly ten million kelvin, hydrogen nuclei begin fusing into helium via the proton-proton chain, releasing energy that halts further contraction. The object has become a star and takes its place on the zero-age main sequence of the Hertzsprung-Russell diagram.

Once a star reaches the main sequence it enters the longest and most stable phase of its existence. In stars of about one solar mass, the dominant energy mechanism is the proton-proton chain, in which pairs of hydrogen nuclei fuse into deuterium, which captures a third proton to form helium-3, and two helium-3 nuclei combine to yield helium-4 and two free protons. In more massive stars, the carbon-nitrogen-oxygen cycle dominates, using carbon as a catalyst while achieving the same net result of converting four protons into one helium-4 nucleus. The energy released by these reactions creates an outward radiation pressure that exactly balances the inward pull of gravity, maintaining the star in hydrostatic equilibrium for millions to billions of years, depending on its mass.

At the core of every main sequence star, the steady fusion of hydrogen builds up a growing reservoir of helium ash. Because helium requires a higher ignition temperature than hydrogen, this accumulating helium core initially remains inert while hydrogen burning continues in a surrounding shell. As the shell advances outward and deposits more helium onto the core, the core contracts and heats under its own weight, and the increased temperature causes the hydrogen-burning shell to accelerate. The released energy inflates the outer layers of the star, which expands to many times its original radius and cools at the surface, becoming redder in colour. The star has become a red giant, and for a star of solar mass this transformation will occur in about five billion years, ending the Sun's main sequence lifetime.

When the helium core reaches temperatures of roughly one hundred million kelvin, helium ignites in a rapid event called the helium flash in low-mass stars, which is invisible externally because it occurs inside a degenerate core and is absorbed by surrounding layers before reaching the surface. The star reorganises its interior and settles onto the horizontal branch, burning helium in the core and hydrogen in a shell simultaneously. This phase lasts approximately one hundred million years. As the helium core is exhausted, the star ascends the asymptotic giant branch and undergoes a series of thermal pulses in which helium ignites episodically, driving strong stellar winds that blow away the outer envelope. The stripped remnant of the core, a white dwarf composed of carbon and oxygen, cools slowly over billions of years with no ongoing fusion.

For stars with initial masses greater than roughly eight times that of the Sun, the stellar life cycle culminates in a spectacular explosion rather than a quiet fading. After hydrogen and helium burning, successive shells of carbon, neon, oxygen, and silicon ignite in an accelerating sequence of nuclear burning stages. Each stage produces heavier elements and releases less energy per reaction, so each phase proceeds faster than the one before it. Silicon burning lasts only about one day and produces an iron-nickel core. Iron represents the endpoint of exothermic nuclear fusion because fusing iron-group nuclei consumes rather than releases energy. When the iron core exceeds the Chandrasekhar limit of approximately one point four solar masses, electron degeneracy pressure can no longer support it, and gravitational collapse begins instantaneously.

Beyond the familiar white dwarf endpoint of low-mass stars, the remnants of massive stellar explosions are among the most exotic objects in the universe. A neutron star packs more than one solar mass into a sphere only twenty kilometres across, achieving densities comparable to atomic nuclei throughout its bulk. The outer crust is composed of increasingly neutron-rich nuclei arranged in a crystalline lattice, while the inner core may contain exotic phases of matter such as hyperons or quark-gluon plasma, though current nuclear physics cannot definitively determine the equation of state at these extreme densities.

When the iron core of a massive star collapses, it shrinks from roughly the size of Earth to a sphere about twenty kilometres in diameter in under one second, during which the density reaches that of atomic nuclei and the strong nuclear force provides a sharp repulsion that halts the collapse. The infalling outer layers rebound off this incompressible neutron core, and an enormous pulse of neutrinos, carrying approximately ten to the power of forty-six joules of energy, helps drive a shockwave outward through the stellar envelope, tearing the star apart in a core-collapse supernova. The newly formed neutron star inherits the angular momentum of the original stellar core compressed into a tiny volume and begins rotating at tens to hundreds of revolutions per second. When such a pulsar is in a binary system and accretes matter from a companion star, angular momentum transfer spins the neutron star up to extraordinary rotational velocities in a process called pulsar recycling. The fastest known millisecond pulsars complete over seven hundred rotations per second, a rate so extreme that any engineering material placed on the surface would be flung away by centrifugal forces exceeding ten billion times the surface gravity of Earth. This rotational energy reservoir powers the pulsar's beamed radio emissions, which sweep the sky like a cosmic lighthouse, detectable from thousands of light-years away.

The magnetic fields of young neutron stars are among the strongest found anywhere in nature, typically reaching ten to the power of twelve gauss at birth, roughly one trillion times stronger than Earth's own field. In isolated pulsars these fields decay gradually and the rotation slows through magnetic dipole radiation, a process measurable as a steady increase in the pulse period accumulating over years of precise timing observations. In recycled millisecond pulsars, by contrast, the prolonged mass accretion phase buries and partially quenches the field, and the magnetic field of a recycled pulsar decays to between one million and one billion gauss, several orders of magnitude below that of a young pulsar of equivalent age. This field weakening is precisely what allows the recycled pulsar to maintain its extraordinary spin rate: a weaker magnetic field produces less rotational braking, preserving the rapid rotation for billions of years rather than the millions characteristic of young energetic pulsars.

The chemical legacy of stellar evolution is imprinted on every planet, organism, and interstellar dust grain in the galaxy. Each generation of stars enriches the surrounding interstellar medium with the products of their nucleosynthesis, gradually raising the metallicity of the gas from which subsequent stars form. Astronomers quantify this enrichment through spectroscopic observations of stellar photospheres, which preserve a record of the chemical composition of the gas cloud from which each star formed, acting as a fossil record of galactic chemical evolution over cosmic time. The steady increase in stellar metallicity across successive stellar generations, from the first metal-poor Population II stars to the metal-rich Population I stars in the disc, is one of the most direct confirmations that the universe began with only hydrogen, helium, and trace lithium, and that all heavier elements were synthesised in stellar interiors and scattered by stellar deaths.
