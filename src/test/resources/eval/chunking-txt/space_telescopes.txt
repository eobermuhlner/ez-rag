Space-based observatories have transformed astronomy by lifting instruments above the distorting and absorbing effects of Earth's atmosphere, granting access to wavelength ranges invisible from the ground and enabling angular resolutions that no ground-based telescope can match without the assistance of adaptive optics or interferometry. From the early orbital experiments of the 1960s to the towering achievements of the Hubble Space Telescope and the James Webb Space Telescope, space astronomy has repeatedly redefined the boundaries of observable cosmic phenomena and produced discoveries that have shaped our understanding of stars, galaxies, exoplanets, and the large-scale structure of the universe.

The Hubble Space Telescope, launched aboard the Space Shuttle Discovery in April 1990, is the most productive scientific instrument ever deployed in orbit. Despite the initial embarrassment of a flawed primary mirror that produced blurred images, the first servicing mission in December 1993 installed corrective optics that restored full design performance. Subsequent servicing missions upgraded the suite of scientific instruments to include the Space Telescope Imaging Spectrograph, the Advanced Camera for Surveys, and the Wide Field Camera 3, each of which opened new scientific capabilities. Hubble's contributions span virtually every area of astrophysics, including the determination of the Hubble constant through Cepheid variable observations, the discovery of the accelerating expansion of the universe in conjunction with ground-based supernova surveys, the characterization of stellar populations in globular clusters, and the imaging of galaxy morphologies at cosmological distances.

The Chandra X-ray Observatory, launched in 1999, operates at photon energies between 0.1 and 10 kiloelectronvolts, a band invisible from the ground because Earth's atmosphere is opaque to X-rays. Chandra's angular resolution of 0.5 arcseconds, achieved through its nested grazing-incidence mirrors, remains unmatched by any other X-ray facility and enables the study of compact objects such as neutron stars, black holes, and white dwarfs with extraordinary spatial detail. X-ray observations reveal the aftermath of supernova explosions in the form of hot expanding shells of gas, the accretion discs around stellar-mass and supermassive black holes, and the diffuse hot intracluster gas in galaxy clusters that constitutes the majority of cluster baryons. Chandra has produced detailed maps of the X-ray emission from clusters like the Bullet Cluster, where the hot gas is displaced from the dark matter halo by ram pressure during a merger, providing some of the most compelling evidence for the particle nature of dark matter.

The Spitzer Space Telescope operated from 2003 to 2020 at infrared wavelengths from 3.6 to 160 micrometres, a range that is strongly absorbed by atmospheric water vapour and carbon dioxide at ground level. Infrared observations penetrate the dust clouds that obscure star-forming regions, revealing the embedded protostars and protoplanetary discs in their earliest stages of assembly. Spitzer made landmark observations of exoplanet atmospheres, measuring the secondary eclipse depths of hot Jupiters at thermal infrared wavelengths and constructing the first temperature maps of tidally locked exoplanets, showing that the peak thermal emission was offset from the substellar point by atmospheric winds transporting heat from the dayside to the nightside. The Spitzer IRAC instrument also played a crucial role in characterising the TRAPPIST-1 planetary system before the James Webb Space Telescope era, providing photometric constraints on the radii and densities of all seven worlds.

The Fermi Gamma-ray Space Telescope, launched in 2008, surveys the sky in gamma rays from 20 megaelectronvolts to more than 300 gigaelectronvolts, detecting the highest-energy photons produced by cosmic sources. The Fermi Large Area Telescope has catalogued thousands of gamma-ray sources including active galactic nuclei, pulsars, supernova remnants, and gamma-ray bursts, and its all-sky survey has revealed the Fermi bubbles, two enormous lobes of gamma-ray emission extending 25,000 light-years above and below the galactic centre that may represent the remnant of past episodes of accretion activity at the Milky Way's central black hole. Pulsars are among the most numerous Fermi sources, with the catalogue containing hundreds of millisecond and young energetic pulsars whose gamma-ray emission constrains models of particle acceleration in pulsar magnetospheres.

The XMM-Newton X-ray observatory, operated by the European Space Agency since 1999 in parallel with Chandra, offers a larger collecting area that compensates for its lower angular resolution, making it particularly well suited to spectroscopic studies of extended sources and faint point sources requiring high photon counts. Together with Chandra, XMM-Newton has enabled detailed spectral analyses of the hot gas in galaxy clusters, constraining the thermodynamic properties of the intracluster medium and the abundance of elements heavier than hydrogen, which record the integrated nucleosynthetic history of the cluster galaxies. The combination of large-area X-ray telescopes and optical weak-lensing surveys has produced cluster mass catalogues that test cosmological models by counting the number of massive structures as a function of redshift, a technique sensitive to the growth rate of structure and hence to the equation of state of dark energy.

The Euclid satellite, launched by the European Space Agency in July 2023, is dedicated to mapping the geometry of the dark universe by measuring the shapes of more than one billion distant galaxies and the three-dimensional distribution of galaxy clustering over a quarter of the sky. Weak gravitational lensing distorts the observed ellipticities of background galaxies in proportion to the projected mass along each line of sight, and a statistical analysis of billions of shape measurements yields a map of the large-scale matter distribution including dark matter. Baryon acoustic oscillations recorded in the galaxy clustering pattern provide a standard ruler that, combined with the lensing measurements, constrains the dark matter density, the total matter density, and the properties of dark energy simultaneously with a precision inaccessible to previous surveys.
