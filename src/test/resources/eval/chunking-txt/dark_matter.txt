One of the most profound open questions in all of physics is the nature of dark matter, an invisible component that outweighs the ordinary atomic matter of stars, gas, and dust by a factor of roughly five to one in the universe as a whole. The evidence for dark matter is overwhelming and comes from multiple independent observational lines spanning galaxy scales, galaxy cluster scales, and cosmological scales, yet every laboratory experiment designed to detect dark matter particles directly has so far returned a null result. This frustrating tension between the strength of the gravitational evidence and the absence of a direct particle detection has driven four decades of increasingly creative theoretical proposals and experimental programmes, none of which has yet yielded a confirmed discovery.

The first strong evidence for dark matter emerged from studies of galaxy rotation curves by Vera Rubin and Kent Ford in the 1970s. According to Newtonian gravity, stars orbiting at large radii from the galactic centre should move more slowly, just as outer planets orbit the Sun more slowly than inner ones in a system where most of the mass is concentrated at the centre. Instead, the rotation curves of spiral galaxies remain flat or even slightly rising at large radii far beyond the visible disc, implying that the gravitational mass continues to increase even where no stars or gas are visible. This discrepancy can be accounted for if galaxies are embedded in extended spherical halos of dark matter whose density falls off as roughly the inverse square of the radius, so that the enclosed mass grows linearly with radius, keeping the rotation speed constant. The flatness of rotation curves has since been confirmed for hundreds of spiral galaxies across a wide range of masses and morphologies.

Galaxy clusters provide an independent and equally compelling line of evidence. The total mass of a galaxy cluster can be inferred in three independent ways: by measuring the velocities of the member galaxies and applying the virial theorem, by modelling the temperature and density distribution of the hot intracluster gas visible in X-ray emission, and by measuring the distortion of background galaxy images by gravitational lensing. All three methods consistently find that the total cluster mass exceeds the sum of stellar masses and hot gas mass by a factor of five to ten, confirming a large dark matter component. The Bullet Cluster, formed by the recent collision of two galaxy clusters, provides the most visually dramatic demonstration: the dark matter halos of the two sub-clusters passed through each other almost without interaction during the collision, while the hot gas was slowed by ram pressure and displaced from the dark matter, producing a clear spatial separation between the mass distribution measured by lensing and the gas distribution measured by X-ray emission.

Cosmological observations, particularly measurements of the cosmic microwave background and the large-scale distribution of galaxies, provide the most precise constraints on the dark matter density. The peaks and troughs in the angular power spectrum of the cosmic microwave background encode the acoustic oscillations of the photon-baryon plasma in the early universe, and their relative amplitudes are exquisitely sensitive to the ratio of dark matter to baryonic matter. Fits to the Planck satellite data yield a matter density parameter of 0.315, of which roughly 0.0493 is baryonic matter and the rest, 0.265, is cold dark matter. The baryon acoustic oscillation scale, imprinted at the same epoch and preserved in the galaxy distribution at late times, provides a geometric standard ruler that independently confirms this picture when combined with supernova distance measurements and galaxy clustering statistics.

The most popular theoretical candidate for dark matter has long been the weakly interacting massive particle, or WIMP, a hypothetical particle with a mass between roughly ten and ten thousand times the proton mass that interacts with ordinary matter through the weak nuclear force. WIMPs are attractive candidates because their relic density, produced through thermal freeze-out in the hot early universe, naturally reproduces the observed dark matter abundance for interaction cross-sections at the weak scale, a coincidence known as the WIMP miracle. Direct detection experiments search for the rare nuclear recoils produced when a dark matter particle scatters off a nucleus in a highly shielded underground detector. Despite reaching sensitivities orders of magnitude beyond the original theoretical predictions, experiments such as LUX-ZEPLIN, PandaX, and XENONnT have found no statistically significant signal, excluding large regions of the WIMP parameter space.

Beyond WIMPs, theorists have proposed a rich landscape of alternative dark matter candidates. Axions, originally introduced to solve the strong CP problem of quantum chromodynamics, are extremely light pseudo-Goldstone bosons that could be produced in the early universe through the misalignment mechanism. Sterile neutrinos oscillate from active neutrinos and could be produced in the hot early universe in sufficient numbers to account for dark matter. Primordial black holes formed in the early universe before nucleosynthesis could contribute to or constitute all of the dark matter without conflicting with constraints on the baryon-to-photon ratio. Each of these candidates predicts distinct observational signatures and requires its own suite of detection experiments, from microwave cavity experiments for axions to X-ray satellite searches for the decay photons of sterile neutrinos.

The small-scale structure of dark matter halos provides an additional frontier for testing dark matter models, because the internal density profiles and substructure populations of halos depend on the properties of the dark matter particle. Collisionless cold dark matter simulations predict density cusps in the centres of dwarf galaxies, scaling as the inverse of the radius, but kinematic observations of stars and gas in many nearby dwarf galaxies indicate flat central density cores rather than cusps. This core-cusp tension can be resolved either by baryonic feedback processes, in which supernova-driven gas outflows repeatedly perturb the gravitational potential and flatten the cusp, or by dark matter with significant self-interactions that transport energy from the outer halo inward. Self-interacting dark matter models require a cross-section-to-mass ratio of between zero point one and ten centimetres squared per gram to reproduce the observed core profiles of dwarf galaxies, a range that is being narrowed by comparing predicted core sizes and shapes to photometric and kinematic data from dwarf galaxy surveys.

Axion searches have experienced a renaissance since improvements in microwave resonator technology enabled detection sensitivities approaching the levels required by theoretical models. The Axion Dark Matter eXperiment, ADMX, and its successors operate large superconducting solenoids tuned to convert axions into microwave photons through the inverse Primakoff effect, exploiting the coupling between axions and photons in a strong magnetic field. Because the axion mass is unknown over many decades in frequency, the experiments must scan across frequencies by tuning the resonant cavity, and each frequency requires sufficient integration time to exceed the noise floor. The QCD axion must have a mass between two and one thousand microelectronvolts to account for the full dark matter density observed by Planck, and current experiments have now probed portions of this range with sufficient sensitivity to rule out the axion if it exists with coupling strengths at the upper end of theoretical predictions.

The next decade promises transformative progress on the dark matter question through a combination of increasingly sensitive direct detection experiments reaching the neutrino floor, next-generation axion experiments scanning broad frequency ranges, precision measurements of dwarf galaxy kinematics and substructure with the Vera Rubin Observatory and the Roman Space Telescope, and gravitational wave detectors sensitive to the mass spectrum of compact object mergers. If dark matter consists of primordial black holes, future microlensing surveys will either confirm or strongly constrain their mass function. If it consists of WIMPs, direct detection experiments will either find them or push the sensitivity past the irreducible background of coherent neutrino-nucleus scattering that mimics the expected WIMP signal. The coming years will either deliver a dark matter discovery or force a deeper revision of our understanding of gravity and cosmology.
