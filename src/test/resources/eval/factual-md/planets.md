# Planets of the Solar System

This document describes the planets of our solar system, including their physical characteristics, moons, atmospheres, and exploration history.

The solar system contains eight recognized planets orbiting the Sun: Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune. Pluto was reclassified as a dwarf planet by the International Astronomical Union in 2006. The four inner planets (Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars) are rocky or terrestrial planets, while the four outer planets (Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune) are giant planets composed primarily of gas or ice.

## Mercury

Mercury is the smallest planet and the one closest to the Sun, completing an orbit every 88 Earth days. Despite its proximity to the Sun, Mercury is not the hottest planet because it has virtually no atmosphere to retain heat. Surface temperatures swing dramatically from about -180 degrees Celsius on the night side to 430 degrees Celsius on the day side. Mercury has a large iron core that takes up about 85 percent of the planet's radius, giving it a surprisingly strong magnetic field for its size. The NASA Mariner 10 spacecraft was the first to visit Mercury in 1974, and MESSENGER orbited the planet from 2011 to 2015, mapping its entire surface and revealing vast plains of solidified lava and thousands of craters. Mercury has no moons.

## Venus

Venus is the second planet from the Sun and is often called Earth's twin because of its similar size and mass, but conditions there are extremely hostile to life. It is the hottest planet in the solar system, with an average surface temperature of about 465 degrees Celsius, hot enough to melt lead, due to a runaway greenhouse effect caused by a thick atmosphere of carbon dioxide. Venus rotates on its axis very slowly and in the opposite direction to most planets, meaning the Sun rises in the west and sets in the east. A day on Venus (243 Earth days) is actually longer than its year (225 Earth days). The Soviet Venera probes successfully landed on Venus in the 1970s and 1980s and transmitted images of a rocky, barren surface before being destroyed by the crushing atmospheric pressure and extreme heat. Venus has no moons.

## Earth

Earth is the third planet from the Sun and the only known world to harbor life. It has a diameter of about 12,742 kilometers and orbits the Sun at an average distance of about 150 million kilometers, a distance defined as one astronomical unit. Earth's atmosphere is composed primarily of nitrogen (78 percent) and oxygen (21 percent). The planet's surface is approximately 71 percent water. Earth has one large natural satellite, the Moon, which stabilizes Earth's axial tilt and drives tidal forces in the oceans. The Moon formed approximately 4.5 billion years ago from debris ejected by a collision between the early Earth and a Mars-sized object called Theia. Earth's magnetic field, generated by the motion of molten iron in its outer core, shields the surface from harmful solar radiation.

## Mars

Mars is the fourth planet from the Sun and is known as the Red Planet because of the iron oxide (rust) on its surface that gives it a distinctive reddish appearance. Mars has a thin atmosphere composed mostly of carbon dioxide, with a surface atmospheric pressure only about 1 percent of Earth's. The planet experiences enormous dust storms that can last for months and cover the entire planet. Mars has the tallest volcano in the solar system, Olympus Mons, which stands about 22 kilometers high — nearly three times the height of Mount Everest. It also has the longest canyon system, Valles Marineris, which stretches about 4,000 kilometers. Mars has two small, irregularly shaped moons, Phobos and Deimos, which are thought to be captured asteroids. NASA's Perseverance rover, which landed on Mars in February 2021, is searching for signs of ancient microbial life and collecting rock samples for potential return to Earth.

## Jupiter

Jupiter is the largest planet in the solar system, with a mass more than twice that of all other planets combined. It is a gas giant composed mostly of hydrogen and helium, with no solid surface. Jupiter is famous for its Great Red Spot, a persistent storm that has been raging for at least 350 years and is large enough to contain multiple Earths. The planet has at least 95 known moons, including the four large Galilean moons discovered by Galileo Galilei in 1610: Io, Europa, Ganymede, and Callisto. Io is the most volcanically active body in the solar system. Europa has a smooth icy surface beneath which scientists believe a liquid water ocean exists, making it one of the most promising locations for extraterrestrial life in the solar system. Ganymede is the largest moon in the solar system, larger even than Mercury. The Cassini spacecraft made a flyby of Jupiter in 2000, and the Juno spacecraft has been orbiting Jupiter since 2016, studying its atmosphere and magnetic field in detail.

## Saturn

Saturn is the sixth planet from the Sun and is instantly recognizable by its spectacular ring system, made of billions of particles of ice and rock ranging in size from tiny grains to chunks as large as houses. The rings extend up to 282,000 kilometers from the planet's center but are remarkably thin — typically less than a kilometer. Like Jupiter, Saturn is a gas giant composed mostly of hydrogen and helium; it is the least dense planet in the solar system, so light that it would float on water if there were an ocean large enough. Saturn has at least 146 known moons, more than any other planet. Titan, Saturn's largest moon, is the only moon in the solar system with a thick atmosphere, and its surface is covered with lakes and rivers of liquid methane and ethane. The Cassini-Huygens mission (1997-2017) orbited Saturn for 13 years and deployed the Huygens probe, which landed on Titan in 2005 and transmitted images of a hazy, cold world.

## Uranus

Uranus is the seventh planet from the Sun and is classified as an ice giant because its interior is thought to contain water, methane, and ammonia ices rather than predominantly hydrogen and helium gas like Jupiter and Saturn. Uranus is unique in the solar system because it rotates on its side: its axial tilt is about 98 degrees relative to its orbit, meaning its poles experience 42 years of continuous sunlight followed by 42 years of darkness. Uranus appears blue-green in color because methane in its upper atmosphere absorbs red light. It has 13 known rings and at least 28 moons, most of which are named after characters from the works of William Shakespeare and Alexander Pope. Voyager 2 is the only spacecraft to have visited Uranus, flying past in January 1986.

## Neptune

Neptune is the eighth and farthest planet from the Sun, completing one orbit every 165 Earth years. It was the first planet to be discovered through mathematical prediction rather than direct observation: in 1846, astronomers Urbain Le Verrier and John Couch Adams independently predicted its location based on perturbations in Uranus's orbit, and Johann Galle confirmed the discovery. Neptune has the strongest sustained winds in the solar system, reaching up to 2,100 kilometers per hour. Like Uranus, Neptune is classified as an ice giant. Its largest moon, Triton, is one of the few moons in the solar system that orbits in the opposite direction to its planet's rotation (retrograde orbit), suggesting it was captured from the Kuiper Belt. Voyager 2 flew past Neptune in 1989, revealing a dynamic atmosphere featuring a Great Dark Spot (a storm that has since dissipated) and active geysers on Triton that shoot nitrogen gas several kilometers into the sky.

## Asteroid Belt and Kuiper Belt

The Asteroid Belt is a region between Mars and Jupiter containing millions of asteroids, rocky bodies left over from the formation of the solar system about 4.6 billion years ago. The largest object in the asteroid belt is Ceres, which is large enough to be classified as a dwarf planet. Despite the vast number of asteroids, the belt is mostly empty space; spacecraft travel through it routinely without colliding with anything. The Dawn spacecraft orbited Vesta (2011-2012) and Ceres (2015-2018), revealing that Ceres has bright salt deposits in craters, possibly formed by ancient water activity.

The Kuiper Belt is a region of the solar system beyond Neptune extending from about 30 to 50 astronomical units from the Sun. It is similar to the asteroid belt but far larger and composed mainly of icy bodies. Pluto, once considered the ninth planet, was reclassified as a dwarf planet in 2006 and is the largest known Kuiper Belt object. NASA's New Horizons spacecraft flew past Pluto in July 2015, revealing a surprisingly complex world with mountains of water ice, a vast nitrogen glacier shaped like a heart (informally named Tombaugh Regio), and a thin atmosphere.

## Voyager Missions

The Voyager missions (Voyager 1 and 2, both launched in 1977) took advantage of a rare alignment of the outer planets to conduct grand tours of the solar system. Voyager 1 flew past Jupiter and Saturn, then continued into interstellar space, becoming the first human-made object to cross the heliopause (the boundary of the Sun's influence) in 2012. Voyager 2 visited all four outer planets — Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune — and remains the only spacecraft to have done so. Both Voyager probes carry golden phonograph records with sounds and images from Earth, intended as messages for any extraterrestrial civilization that might find them.
