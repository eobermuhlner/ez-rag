This document describes cooking techniques, methods, and culinary traditions from around the world.

Cooking is the art and science of preparing food using heat, time, and technique. Human cooking is thought to have begun over a million years ago and has been central to our physical and social development. The application of heat changes the chemical composition of food: proteins denature and coagulate, starches gelatinize, sugars caramelize, and complex flavor compounds form through the Maillard reaction. Different cooking methods produce very different results from the same ingredients, which is why technique is as important as ingredients in professional cooking.

Boiling involves heating food in liquid, typically water, at 100 degrees Celsius at sea level. This is used for cooking pasta, vegetables, potatoes, eggs, grains, and legumes. Boiling can leach water-soluble vitamins and minerals from vegetables into the cooking water, which is why steaming or roasting often preserves more nutrients. The cooking liquid from boiling becomes stock or broth and can be used as a base for soups and sauces.

Simmering is similar to boiling but uses lower temperatures (around 85-96 degrees Celsius), producing gentle, small bubbles. It is used for slow-cooking tough cuts of meat, making stocks and stews, and developing deep flavors. Long simmering breaks down collagen in meat into gelatin, making braised dishes tender and rich. Many classic preparations like beef bourguignon and coq au vin rely on extended simmering.

Steaming cooks food using steam generated from boiling water, without submerging the food directly in the liquid. This method is particularly beneficial for delicate foods like fish, dumplings, and vegetables because it preserves their color, texture, nutrients, and natural flavors. Steaming is fundamental in Chinese, Japanese, and Southeast Asian cuisines, where bamboo steamers layered over woks are used to cook dim sum, rice, and vegetables. A pressure cooker uses pressurized steam to cook food faster than conventional methods.

Baking uses dry heat in an enclosed oven environment, typically between 150 and 250 degrees Celsius, to cook food. The dry heat causes moisture to evaporate from the food's surface, creating a crust or browning effect. Baking is essential for bread, cakes, cookies, pies, pastries, and casseroles. The science of baking is precise: small changes in the ratio of ingredients can significantly affect the final texture. Leavening agents such as yeast, baking powder, and baking soda are used to create gas bubbles that make baked goods light and airy.

Roasting uses dry oven heat to cook larger pieces of meat, poultry, and vegetables. Unlike baking, roasting typically uses higher temperatures and often involves fat. The exterior of roasted food develops a deeply browned, flavorful crust due to the Maillard reaction and caramelization, while the interior cooks more gently. Roasting vegetables concentrates their natural sugars. A roast chicken, for example, is placed uncovered in a hot oven so that the skin becomes crispy while the meat remains juicy.

Grilling cooks food over direct heat from below, producing characteristic grill marks and a smoky, charred flavor. Gas and charcoal grills are the most common types. High heat seals the surface quickly, creating a flavorful crust. Grilling is popular for steaks, burgers, fish fillets, vegetables, and skewered meats. In many cultures, grilling is associated with outdoor cooking: American barbecue, Brazilian churrasco, and South African braai are all grilling traditions.

Frying involves cooking food in hot oil. Deep frying fully submerges food in oil at temperatures between 175 and 200 degrees Celsius, producing a crispy exterior. The water in the food rapidly evaporates, preventing oil absorption into the interior and creating a light, crunchy crust. French fries, fried chicken, doughnuts, and tempura are classic deep-fried foods. Pan frying uses a shallow layer of oil in a pan and is used for items like sautéed vegetables, pan-seared fish, and shallow-fried cutlets. Stir-frying, used extensively in Asian cuisines, involves cooking small pieces of food quickly in a very hot wok with a small amount of oil while constantly stirring.

Braising is a combination cooking method that starts with searing meat at high heat to develop a brown crust, then adding liquid and cooking slowly in a covered vessel at low heat. This technique is ideal for tough, collagen-rich cuts of meat such as beef short ribs, lamb shanks, and pork shoulder, which become tender and flavorful after hours of slow, moist cooking. The braising liquid reduces and concentrates into a rich sauce. Classic braised dishes include osso buco (Italian braised veal shanks) and pot roast.

Smoking is a cooking method that uses smoke from burning wood to flavor and preserve food. Cold smoking (below 32 degrees Celsius) flavors but does not cook food, while hot smoking (between 52 and 80 degrees Celsius) both flavors and cooks. Different types of wood produce distinctive flavors: hickory and mesquite are assertive, while apple and cherry woods are milder and sweeter. American barbecue traditions center on slow smoking of meats like brisket, ribs, and pulled pork over many hours. Salmon, trout, cheese, and bacon are also commonly smoked.

Fermentation is one of the oldest food preservation methods, using microorganisms like bacteria, yeasts, and molds to transform food through biochemical processes. Fermentation produces characteristic flavors, improves nutritional value, and extends shelf life. Bread rises because yeast ferments sugars in dough to produce carbon dioxide. Cheese, yogurt, and kefir are made through bacterial fermentation of milk. Wine and beer are produced by yeast fermentation of fruit or grain sugars. Kimchi, sauerkraut, and other fermented vegetables are preserved through lactic acid fermentation. Traditional soy sauce and miso are fermented for months or years to develop complex umami flavors.

Sous vide is a modern cooking technique in which food is vacuum-sealed in a bag and cooked at a precisely controlled low temperature in a water bath for an extended period. The method was developed in the 1970s by French chef Georges Pralus and scientist Bruno Goussault and has become standard in professional kitchens. Sous vide cooking produces exceptionally consistent results because food never exceeds the target temperature. A steak cooked sous vide at 54 degrees Celsius for one to four hours will be perfectly medium-rare throughout, without the gradient of doneness from edge to center typical of traditional cooking. The food is typically seared in a hot pan after the water bath to develop a brown crust.

French cuisine has had an enormous influence on the culinary world through codified sauces, classical techniques, and the concept of haute cuisine. The five French mother sauces form the basis of classical French cooking. French cuisine gave the world croissants, baguettes, crème brûlée, soufflés, and coq au vin. Auguste Escoffier, the 19th-century French chef, systematized French cooking in his Le Guide Culinaire and established the brigade system of kitchen organization still used today.

Italian cuisine is characterized by its simplicity, regionality, and emphasis on high-quality ingredients. Italian food varies dramatically by region: northern Italy emphasizes butter, cream, and risotto, while southern Italy uses olive oil, tomatoes, and dried pasta. Pasta, which exists in hundreds of shapes matched to specific sauces, is central to Italian cuisine. Pizza originated in Naples in the 18th century as a simple peasant bread with toppings. Italian contributions to world cuisine include espresso, gelato, prosciutto, Parmigiano-Reggiano cheese, and extra-virgin olive oil.

Japanese cuisine (washoku) is recognized by UNESCO as an Intangible Cultural Heritage and is known for its emphasis on seasonality, presentation, and umami flavor. Core principles include using fresh, seasonal ingredients and allowing their natural flavors to shine. Key fermented ingredients form the foundation of Japanese flavor. Sushi combines vinegared rice with raw or cooked ingredients. Ramen, a noodle soup dish, has regional variations ranging from salty shio ramen to rich tonkotsu (pork bone broth) ramen. Japanese knife-making and professional kitchen skills are renowned worldwide.

Indian cuisine is extraordinarily diverse, with distinct regional traditions across the subcontinent shaped by geography, religion, and cultural history. Spices are central to Indian cooking: turmeric, cumin, coriander, cardamom, fenugreek, mustard seeds, and chile peppers are used in countless combinations. The concept of a curry refers not to a single dish but to a broad category of sauce-based dishes seasoned with a blend of spices. Tandoor clay ovens are used to cook naan bread and tandoori chicken at very high temperatures. Dal (lentil soup) and rice are dietary staples across much of India, while coastal regions have rich seafood traditions.
