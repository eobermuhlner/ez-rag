This document describes chemical elements from the periodic table, including their properties, atomic numbers, symbols, and uses.

Chemical elements are pure substances made of one type of atom. Each element is defined by its atomic number, which is the number of protons in the nucleus. The periodic table, first organized by Dmitri Mendeleev in 1869, arranges elements by atomic number and groups elements with similar chemical properties into columns called groups. There are 118 confirmed elements, of which 94 occur naturally on Earth.

Hydrogen is the lightest and most abundant element in the universe, making up about 75 percent of all ordinary matter by mass. It has atomic number 1 and chemical symbol H. At room temperature and pressure, hydrogen is a colorless, odorless, diatomic gas (H₂). Stars like the Sun produce energy by fusing hydrogen nuclei into helium in a process called nuclear fusion. On Earth, hydrogen is essential for life: it is a component of water (H₂O) and all organic molecules. Hydrogen is used in the industrial production of ammonia via the Haber process, in petroleum refining, and increasingly as a clean fuel in hydrogen fuel cells.

Helium is the second lightest element and has atomic number 2 with symbol He. It is the second most abundant element in the observable universe but is relatively rare on Earth, where it is found primarily in natural gas deposits. Helium is a noble gas, meaning it does not react chemically under ordinary conditions. It has the lowest boiling point of any element (-269 degrees Celsius), just 4 degrees above absolute zero. Helium is used to cool the superconducting magnets in MRI machines and particle accelerators, as a lifting gas for balloons and airships (being non-flammable unlike hydrogen), and as an inert atmosphere for arc welding.

Lithium has atomic number 3 and symbol Li. It is the lightest metal and the least dense solid element. Lithium is highly reactive and must be stored in oil to prevent it from reacting with air and water. It is used extensively in rechargeable lithium-ion batteries that power smartphones, laptops, and electric vehicles. Lithium compounds are also used as mood-stabilizing medications in the treatment of bipolar disorder. Chile, Australia, and China hold the world's largest reserves of lithium.

Carbon has atomic number 6 and symbol C. It is the fourth most abundant element in the universe by mass and the chemical basis of all known life on Earth. Carbon forms the backbone of all biological molecules, including proteins, carbohydrates, lipids, and nucleic acids. It can form millions of different compounds due to its ability to form four stable covalent bonds. Carbon exists in several allotropes: graphite (used as a lubricant and in pencil leads), diamond (the hardest natural material, used in cutting tools and jewelry), and fullerenes and graphene (used in advanced materials research). Carbon-14, a radioactive isotope with a half-life of about 5,730 years, is used in radiocarbon dating to determine the age of organic materials.

Nitrogen has atomic number 7 and symbol N. It makes up approximately 78 percent of Earth's atmosphere by volume, making it the most abundant gas in air. Molecular nitrogen (N₂) is extremely stable and relatively unreactive under normal conditions due to its strong triple bond. The Haber-Bosch process, developed in the early 20th century, converts atmospheric nitrogen into ammonia (NH₃), which is essential for producing nitrogen fertilizers that support modern agriculture. Without artificial nitrogen fixation, Earth could not support its current human population. Liquid nitrogen, which boils at -196 degrees Celsius, is used for cryogenic preservation of biological samples, rapid food freezing, and cooling in scientific research.

Oxygen has atomic number 8 and symbol O. It makes up about 21 percent of Earth's atmosphere and is essential for the survival of most living organisms. Oxygen is the most abundant element in Earth's crust, composing about 46 percent by mass when combined in minerals and water. Photosynthesis in plants and algae produces oxygen as a byproduct of converting carbon dioxide and water into glucose. Most living things use oxygen in cellular respiration to break down food molecules and extract energy. Liquid oxygen is used as an oxidizer in rocket propellants, combining with liquid hydrogen to produce the high-thrust engines of space launch vehicles. Ozone (O₃), a molecule of three oxygen atoms, forms a layer in the stratosphere that absorbs harmful ultraviolet radiation from the Sun.

Silicon has atomic number 14 and symbol Si. It is the second most abundant element in Earth's crust (after oxygen) and is found in sand, quartz, and many minerals as silicon dioxide. Silicon is a metalloid, meaning it has properties intermediate between metals and nonmetals. Its semiconductor properties make it the fundamental material of modern electronics: nearly all computer chips, transistors, solar cells, and integrated circuits are made from highly purified silicon. Silicon Valley in California was named for the silicon chip industry that grew there. Sand and quartz are primary sources of silicon for the semiconductor industry.

Sodium has atomic number 11 and symbol Na (from the Latin natrium). It is a soft, silvery-white metal that reacts violently with water, producing hydrogen gas and sodium hydroxide. Sodium is essential for life: it regulates fluid balance, nerve function, and muscle contraction in living organisms. The most familiar sodium compound is sodium chloride (NaCl), common table salt, which has been essential to human civilization for food preservation and flavoring. Sodium is used in streetlamps (sodium vapor lamps produce a characteristic yellow light), in the production of various chemicals, and in liquid metal form as a coolant in some nuclear reactors.

Aluminum (or aluminium) has atomic number 13 and symbol Al. It is the most abundant metal in Earth's crust and the third most abundant element overall. Despite its abundance, pure aluminum was once more valuable than gold because it was difficult to extract; the top of the Washington Monument was capped with aluminum in 1884 as a precious metal. The development of the Hall-Héroult electrolytic process in 1886 made aluminum production practical and affordable. Aluminum is lightweight, corrosion-resistant, and highly conductive, making it invaluable in aircraft construction, beverage cans, electrical transmission lines, and packaging. Recycling aluminum requires only about 5 percent of the energy needed to produce it from ore.

Iron has atomic number 26 and symbol Fe (from the Latin ferrum). It is the most abundant element on Earth by mass, making up most of the planet's solid inner and liquid outer core, and the fourth most abundant element in Earth's crust. Iron is a transition metal with strong magnetic properties; the Earth's magnetic field is generated by convection of molten iron in the outer core. Iron has been central to human technological development since the Iron Age began around 1200 BC. Steel, an alloy of iron with carbon (and often other elements), is the most important structural material in construction, manufacturing, and infrastructure. Hemoglobin, the protein in red blood cells that carries oxygen through the blood, contains iron atoms as its oxygen-binding centers.

Copper has atomic number 29 and symbol Cu (from the Latin cuprum, named for the island of Cyprus). Copper was one of the first metals used by humans, and the Copper Age precedes the Bronze Age. It is the best electrical conductor among common metals and is used extensively in electrical wiring, motors, and electronic circuit boards. Copper also has excellent thermal conductivity and is used in heat exchangers and cookware. The Statue of Liberty is covered in approximately 80 tons of copper sheet. Copper has natural antimicrobial properties; copper surfaces in hospitals can help reduce bacterial contamination. Bronze (an alloy of copper and tin) and brass (copper and zinc) are important industrial alloys.

Gold has atomic number 79 and symbol Au (from the Latin aurum). It is a dense, soft, shiny metal that is highly resistant to corrosion and chemical attack; it does not tarnish, rust, or dissolve in most acids. Gold has been valued throughout history for jewelry, coinage, and as a store of wealth. The most important historical gold rushes occurred in California (1848), Australia (1851), and South Africa (1886), each dramatically affecting regional and global economics. In modern times, gold is used in electronics because of its excellent conductivity and corrosion resistance: it is used to plate electrical connectors in computers and smartphones. South Africa, Australia, Russia, and China are the world's largest producers of gold.

Uranium has atomic number 92 and symbol U. It is a heavy, radioactive metal that is the heaviest naturally occurring element. Uranium has three naturally occurring isotopes: U-238 (the most abundant at 99.27 percent), U-235 (0.72 percent), and U-234. The fission of U-235 and plutonium-239 is the basis of nuclear power and nuclear weapons. Natural uranium must be enriched to increase the proportion of U-235 before it can sustain a nuclear chain reaction in most reactor designs. Nuclear power plants currently provide about 10 percent of global electricity. Uranium was discovered in 1789 and named after the planet Uranus, which had recently been discovered.
